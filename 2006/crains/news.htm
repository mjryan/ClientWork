Plan for 2016 Olympics disclosed

Ryan: 'We're not going to build ordinary venues'

(Crain's) � Arguing that Chicago is uniquely equipped to host an "athlete-centric" 2016 Olympics, officials have sketched out plans to scatter venues for 33 sports throughout a wide swath of the central part of the city.

Proposed in a bold effort to both involve neighborhoods and impress visitors is everything from tennis and whitewater rafting on the North Side lakefront to gymnastics and swimming on the Near West Side, judo at McCormick Place and track-and-field competition in Washington Park.

For the first time, officials also began to put some numbers on how much it would cost to build housing and sports facilities for athletes from around the world, with indications the ultimate price tag will approach or exceed $2 billion.

"We're not going to build ordinary venues. Chicagoans aspire to greatness," said former Aon Corp. CEO Patrick Ryan, who heads Olympic planning efforts for Mayor Richard M. Daley.

While Chicago will maintain its Midwest pragmatism, "We've been focused on winning spectacular games. We won't win without (that)," given stiff competition from San Francisco, Los Angeles and numerous foreign cities that are bidding for the games, Mr. Ryan added.

The plan, crafted by architects from Skidmore Owings & Merrill LLP, developers at Jones Lang LaSalle Inc. and a team of a dozen other planning experts and officially released Saturday morning, is based on using Chicago's existing resources of an open lakefront, a tight urban core and solid public transportation.

Thanks to those advantages, they said, almost all venues can be concentrated in four or five clumps surrounding the Loop, with a third of the athletes able to be housed within a five-minute drive of their sport venues, and 85% within 15 minutes away .

As a result, Chicago is proposing what would be the most geographically compact Olympics in at least 20 years, said Doug Arnot, a city consultant who previously worked on the 1996 Atlanta Olympics and on World Cup soccer games.

The first, smallest clump would be in the North Side's Lincoln Park. Montrose Harbor would be used for sailing, a rebuilt tennis complex at Waveland Avenue for that sport, and North Avenue Beach for volleyball.

In addition, an outmoded rowing area just south of Fullerton Avenue likely would be converted to use for whitewater sports if officials conclude the facility would be economically viable after the games conclude.

The second, largest grouping would be in the Soldier Field-McCormick Place-Burnham Park area on the Near South Side, where sports from wrestling to weight lifting, volleyball and soccer would take place, and where the international broadcast center would be located.

Most of that would require only minor renovations, officials said, but a stadium for bicycle racing would be built on Northerly Island, and portions of the Soldier Field wall might have to be temporarily removed to expand the field to regulation soccer size.

The Near South Side also would be the location of the Olympic Village, home to 17,000 competitors during the games. The housing complex, which officials want converted to about 4,000 private apartments after the games, would be constructed over a 37-acre truck parking lot located immediately south of McCormick Place.


Rendering of Skidmore Owings & Merrill's preliminary vision for the Olympic Village, looking southwest.

On the Near West Side, the United Center would host high-profile gymnastics and basketball competition, but the major improvement would be construction of a large aquatic complex on the University of Illinois at Chicago campus, consisting of several pools and a diving area. Officials said the university has expressed interest in renovating or replacing its existing pool area.

As disclosed earlier in the week, Washington Park on the mid-South Side would be home to a largely temporary 95,000-seat stadium for opening and closing ceremonies and track-and-field events. Officials say the historic park will be renovated the process, with the stadium cut back to 10,000 seats later for neighborhood use.

Mr. Ryan declined to issue an overall cost estimate for construction, saying plans are in development, but he pegged the Olympic Village at about $1 billion, and the Washington Park stadium at about $300 million.

He also stressed that much or all of the $1 billion for the Olympic Village could be recouped when the housing is converted to public use. He indicated the stadium will include skyboxes that could defray much of the cost of that structure and said most facilities would be extra large to encourage extra attendance and ticket revenues.

Based on those numbers, overall construction costs for the 33 sports venues and Olympic Village "easily" will approach $2 billion and "probably be greater," said Marc Ganis, a Chicago-based stadium consultant.

Mr. Ganis stressed that those are gross figures-prior to, for instance, proceeds from selling Olympic Village apartments later-and Mayor Daley has said all money will be privately raised and not come from taxpayers.

Mr. Ryan would not disclose who is paying for the city's Olympic bid, which reportedly could cost as much as $25 million by next March. "I haven't thought of that," he said. "We'll comply with all the required filings." TheChicago Tribune reported Friday that Mr. Ryan's group has raised $8 million of the $25 million.

Mr. Ryan did say his Olympics planning group would seek needed formal approvals from the Chicago Park District and Chicago Plan Commission. Under plans released so far, all land used for the Olympics already is owned by the government, mostly the park district and Metropolitan Pier and Exposition Authority, which operates McCormick Place.

The details released Saturday morning were sent to the U.S. Olympic Committee Friday. USOC says it will decide by next March which U.S. city, if any, will compete against international rivals for rights to host the 2016 games.
